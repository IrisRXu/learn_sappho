Peom 31

He seems to me equal to gods that man
whoever he is who opposite you
sits and listens close
to your sweet speaking
and lovely laughing—oh it
puts the heart in my chest on wings
for when I look at you, even a moment, no speaking
is left in me
no: tongue breaks and thin
fire is racing under skin
and in eyes no sight and drumming
fills ears
and cold sweat holds me and shaking
grips me all, greener than grass
I am and dead—or almost
I seem to me.

Analysis

Click on any line to see its literary analysis

Hyperbole comparing mortal to divine, establishing the poem's theme of extraordinary experience.

Use of deixis ("whoever") creates ambiguity while "opposite you" establishes spatial dynamics.

Active verbs ("sits and listens") create immediacy and intimacy in the scene.

Alliteration ("sweet speaking") emphasizes the sensual nature of the voice.

Dash creates dramatic pause; "lovely laughing" uses alliteration to convey joy.

Metaphor of heart "on wings" conveys emotional uplift and destabilization.

Temporal marker "when" shifts to immediate present; emphasizes cause and effect.

Short phrase emphasizes total loss of speech through its brevity.

Colon and fragmentary syntax mirror the breaking of speech described.

Vivid metaphor of "fire racing" captures intensity of physical sensation.

Asyndeton (no conjunctions) creates rushing effect of symptoms.

Brief phrase emphasizes overwhelming sensory experience.

Physical symptoms build through parallel structure ("cold sweat holds...shaking grips").

Simile "greener than grass" describes pallor through natural imagery.

Dash creates dramatic pause before climactic statement of death-like state.

Self-reflexive ending ("seem to me") questions reality of experience.
