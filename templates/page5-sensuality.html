Fragment 31 - Complete

The famous "jealousy poem" where Sappho describes physical symptoms of emotional intensity that mirror both passionate desire and proximity to death.

He seems to me equal to gods that man
whoever he is who opposite you
sits and listens close
to your sweet speaking
and lovely laughing—oh it
puts the heart in my chest on wings
for when I look at you, even a moment, no speaking
is left in me
no: tongue breaks and thin
fire is racing under skin
and in eyes no sight and drumming
fills ears
and cold sweat holds me and shaking
grips me all, greener than grass
I am and dead—or almost
I seem to me.

Analysis

The opening lines establish a scene of observation and comparison. The speaker positions herself as an outsider looking in, comparing the man to gods—a common trope in Greek poetry for expressing admiration.

The focus shifts to the object of desire, emphasizing the physical proximity and intimacy of the conversation. The repetition of "you" creates a sense of direct address and personal connection.

The physical symptoms described are intense and overwhelming, creating a powerful metaphor for the experience of desire. The imagery of fire, cold sweat, and shaking suggests both passion and fear.
