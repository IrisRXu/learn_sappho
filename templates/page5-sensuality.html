ECHOES OF SAPPHO

Fragment 31 - Complete

The famous "jealousy poem" where Sappho describes physical symptoms of emotional intensity that mirror both passionate desire and proximity to death.

He seems to me equal to gods that man
whoever he is who opposite you
sits and listens close
to your sweet speaking
and lovely laughing — oh it
puts the heart in my chest on wings
for when I look at you, even a moment, no speaking
is left in me
no: tongue breaks and thin
fire is racing under skin
and in eyes no sight and drumming
fills ears
and cold sweat holds me and shaking
grips me all, greener than grass
I am and dead — or almost
I seem to me.

Translation by Anne Carson, "If Not, Winter: Fragments of Sappho" (2002)

Literary Analysis

Click on any line of the poem for a detailed analysis of the rhetorical devices and techniques used by Sappho.

Comparative Imagery Divine Reference

He seems to me equal to gods that man

This opening line establishes a triangular relationship between Sappho, her beloved, and "that man." The comparison to gods immediately elevates the emotional experience to a divine plane, suggesting transcendence.

The phrase "seems to me" establishes subjectivity from the outset, suggesting that this extreme emotional state alters perception itself—a theme that continues throughout the poem.

Metaphor Motion Imagery

puts the heart in my chest on wings

This vivid metaphor introduces the physical symptoms that will dominate the remainder of the poem. The heart growing wings suggests both elevation and agitation, combining positive and negative aspects of the emotional experience.

The image of wings anticipates the later sensation of trembling, creating a progression from flutter to paralysis that tracks the movement from excitement to overwhelm.

Sensory Imagery Fire Metaphor

fire is racing under skin

The fire imagery represents both desire and fever—again uniting the sensual with symptoms of illness. The verb "racing" adds urgency and uncontrollability to the experience.

This line introduces temperature as a dimension of the experience, which will be contrasted with the "cold sweat" that follows, creating a bodily experience of extremes.

Paradox Central Theme

I am and dead — or almost

This line represents the climax of the poem and embodies the central paradox of Sappho's poetics: the simultaneous experience of being and non-being. The construction juxtaposes existence with death.

The conjunction "and" rather than "but" suggests simultaneity rather than contrast—she is both alive and dead at once, not one state giving way to another. This captures the core of how sensuality and death intertwine in Sappho's poetic vision.

The qualification "or almost" introduces uncertainty that mirrors the liminal state being described—not fully alive, not fully dead, but suspended between states in a transformative moment of intensity.
