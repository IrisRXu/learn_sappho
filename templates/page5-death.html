ECHOES OF SAPPHO

Fragment 95 - Contemplation

An exploration of mortality and the afterlife through the lens of divine privilege, questioning why the gods keep immortality for themselves.

Death is an evil; the gods have so judged;
had it been good, they would die.

Translation adapted from various scholars

Literary Analysis

Click on any line of the poem for a detailed analysis of the rhetorical devices and techniques used by Sappho.

Aphorism Divine Reference

Death is an evil; the gods have so judged;

This line employs aphoristic statement—a concise, memorable assertion that presents a truth about existence. The declaration "Death is an evil" is presented as objective fact rather than personal opinion.

By attributing this judgment to the gods, Sappho employs divine authority to strengthen her claim, while simultaneously setting up the subversive logic of the second line.

The use of semicolon creates a pause that emphasizes the weight of this declaration before transitioning to the evidence that follows.

Logical Inversion Counterfactual

had it been good, they would die.

This line employs counterfactual reasoning, presenting a hypothetical scenario that reveals the inconsistency in divine behavior. The logical structure "if X were true, then Y would occur" creates a powerful syllogism.

The brevity of the line enhances its impact, delivering a subtle but profound critique of divine privilege through understatement rather than direct accusation.

The implied conclusion—that gods selfishly keep immortality for themselves—reveals Sappho's skeptical approach to religious orthodoxy, suggesting mortals are unfairly subjected to death.
